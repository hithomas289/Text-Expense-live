I Tried 5 Expense Tracking Apps So You Don't Have To

Three weeks ago, I had a shoebox problem.

Not literally a shoebox‚ÄîI'm not a monster‚Äîbut a spiritual shoebox. Receipts in my camera roll. Receipts in my email. Receipts crumpled in my jacket pocket from a client lunch I'd already forgotten about.

Tax season was coming. I needed a system. So I did what any reasonable person would do: I tested every expense tracking method I could find.

Five approaches. Three weeks. One clear winner.

Here's what I learned.

Method 1: The Google Sheets Template

Cost: Free
Time to set up: 20 minutes
Time per receipt: 3-5 minutes

I started where most people start‚Äîa spreadsheet.

Google Sheets has a surprisingly decent expense tracker template built right in. Open Sheets, click "Template gallery," select "Monthly budget," and you're off. Columns for date, vendor, amount, category. Automatic totals. A little pie chart that makes you feel organized.

For about four days, I was disciplined. Every receipt got logged. Every category got assigned. I felt like someone who has their life together.

Then life happened.

A busy Tuesday. A working lunch I forgot to log. A coffee receipt I photographed but never entered. By day seven, I had a backlog. By day ten, I'd stopped entirely.

The problem with spreadsheets isn't the spreadsheet. It's the manual data entry. You have to enter expense details manually, making the whole expense tracking activity time-consuming and prone to errors. Every receipt requires you to type the date, the amount, the vendor, the category. That's maybe 90 seconds per receipt‚Äîwhich doesn't sound like much until you have 47 receipts to catch up on.

Google Sheets also doesn't connect to bank accounts natively. So you're flying blind between your actual spending and what you've bothered to log.

The verdict: Great for people who are already organized. Terrible for people who need to become organized. The discipline required is the entire problem.

Method 2: Expensify

Cost: $5/month (Collect plan) or free with limits
Time to set up: 45 minutes
Time per receipt: 30-60 seconds

Expensify is the name everyone knows. Over 15 million people use it. It's been around since the early days of expense apps. It integrates with everything.

I downloaded it expecting to be impressed.

The receipt scanning is genuinely good. Point your phone camera at a receipt, and the OCR pulls out the vendor, amount, and date automatically. Reviewers indicate Expensify streamlines expense tracking, receipt management, and reimbursement, making it easier to submit, approve, and process expenses quickly.

But here's where it gets complicated.

Expensify wants to be everything to everyone. Corporate cards. Travel booking. Team chat. Approval workflows. Bill pay. For a freelancer who just wants to track receipts for tax season, it's like using a sledgehammer to hang a picture frame.

Some users experience a learning curve and occasional navigation difficulties, especially on the web version. The setup took me the better part of an hour‚Äîcreating expense categories, understanding the difference between "workspaces" and "reports," figuring out how to actually export my data.

Expensify can lag in offering advanced features compared to competitors and is best suited for companies that prioritize efficiency and straightforward expense tracking. But for individual freelancers? Costs can quickly add up depending on active members and usage.

I also hit a few occasions where the receipt scanning tool malfunctioned and misread a receipt, causing me to enter the information manually.

The verdict: If you're a company with employees who need expense reports and approval workflows, Expensify makes sense. If you're one person who just wants receipts organized, it's overkill. And that overkill creates friction. Friction kills habits.

Method 3: Zoho Expense

Cost: Starting at $4/user/month
Time to set up: 30 minutes
Time per receipt: 30-60 seconds

I tried Zoho because it kept appearing in "best expense apps" lists at a lower price point than Expensify.

The pitch is similar: scan receipts, auto-extract data, organize everything, generate reports. Users appreciate the affordable pricing and free trial.

And honestly? It's solid.

Reviewers appreciate Zoho Expense's expense tracking capabilities. They find it easy to track expenses by project, differentiate between company card and reimbursable expenses.

One user switching from manual spreadsheets summed it up well: Previously, employees submitted their expenses using a manual Excel spreadsheet. This process was not only time-consuming‚Äîrequiring them to scan each receipt and attach it individually to an email‚Äîbut it also resulted in frequent errors and data quality issues.

But Zoho has the same fundamental issue as Expensify: it's built for teams and businesses, not individuals.

Some users report that costs rise with more users. And the interface, while cleaner than Expensify's, still requires learning. Users find the learning curve steep for Zoho Expense, requiring time and effort to fully understand the platform.

I also ran into a frustrating quirk: Not having the ability to recognize 2 receipts in one picture. If you're at a conference and you have a stack of receipts, you're photographing each one individually.

The verdict: A more affordable, slightly simpler alternative to Expensify. Still designed for businesses with teams. Still requires more setup than most freelancers will tolerate.

Method 4: Shoeboxed

Cost: $29-89/month (or $23-71/month annual)
Time to set up: 15 minutes
Time per receipt: Varies

Shoeboxed takes a completely different approach: they'll scan your physical receipts for you.

Seriously. They send you a prepaid envelope‚Äîthey call it the "Magic Envelope"‚Äîand you stuff your receipts in it and mail them. Their team scans everything and uploads it to your account.

Clear your desks and drawers of paper receipts by mailing them straight to our processing facility for free with our postage-paid Magic Envelope.

For the right person, this is genius. If you're sitting on years of unorganized paper receipts, Shoeboxed will literally do the work for you.

Users can choose to either scan receipts on the go using their smartphones or consolidate a backlog of paper receipts by sending them in for professional processing.

The human verification is a nice touch too: Shoeboxed is the only receipt scanning service with human verification, ensuring accurate data every time.

But there are catches.

First, the price. The platform costs anywhere from $29 to $89 monthly. That's real money‚Äî$350 to $1,000+ per year‚Äîfor receipt tracking.

Second, the mail-in approach creates a lag. You're not tracking expenses in real-time; you're batching them. If you're trying to build a habit of capturing receipts as they happen, mailing envelopes every few weeks doesn't reinforce that behavior.

Third, according to reviews on Google Play store, the free Shoeboxed app leaves a little to be desired; the images it captures aren't crystal clear and can take several shots to focus correctly.

The verdict: Perfect for clearing a backlog of paper receipts. Too expensive and too slow for ongoing, real-time expense tracking. Also not practical for anyone who primarily gets digital receipts‚Äîwhich is most of us now.

Method 5: TextExpense

Cost: $2.99-4.99/month
Time to set up: 30 seconds
Time per receipt: 30 seconds

Full disclosure: this is the one I kept using.

Here's the entire setup process: I texted a photo of a receipt to WhatsApp. That's it. No app download. No account creation. No expense categories to configure.

Thirty seconds later, I got back a message confirming the receipt was processed. Date, vendor, amount, category‚Äîall extracted automatically.

At the end of the week, I requested my Excel report. One tap. Clean spreadsheet with every receipt organized, plus clickable links to the original images.

The thing that made TextExpense stick where everything else failed was the zero friction.

I already check WhatsApp constantly. There's no separate app to open, no login to remember, no interface to navigate. The receipt capture happens in the same place I'm texting my friends and family.

When I bought lunch for a client meeting, I photographed the receipt before I left the restaurant and sent it while walking to my car. Done. Captured. Never thought about it again until I needed the report.

That's the habit that none of the other tools could create: capturing receipts in the moment, without any additional steps or context-switching.

The pricing is also notably lower. Under $5/month versus $29-89 for Shoeboxed or the complexity tax of enterprise-focused tools like Expensify.

The verdict: If you're a freelancer, gig worker, or small business owner who just wants receipts organized without learning new software, this is the answer. The WhatsApp approach means you're using a tool you already know. The friction is essentially zero.
