The Reimbursement Game

Here's how it goes at my company:

You pay for something with your own money‚Äîa client lunch, an Uber to the airport, supplies for a project. You're supposed to save the receipt, log it in the expense system, and submit for reimbursement.

Simple in theory.

In reality: the receipt goes in your pocket, then your wallet, then a pile on your desk, then... somewhere. The expense system requires seventeen fields and a photo upload that never seems to work on mobile. And by the time the deadline hits, you're reconstructing your spending from bank statements and memory.

Last quarter I left $340 on the table. Expenses I paid for, couldn't prove, didn't get back.

That's my money. Gone. Because of receipts.

The Sunday Night Scramble

You know the scramble.

Expense deadline is Monday. It's Sunday night. You're digging through your camera roll looking for that photo you maybe took of the dinner receipt three weeks ago.

Was it before or after the sunset photo? Scroll. Scroll. Scroll.

There it is‚Äîblurry, barely readable, timestamp doesn't match the date you remember.

Now multiply that by fifteen expenses across a month.

This is how I spend my Sundays. Unpaid admin work so I can get paid back for money I already spent.

What Changed

I started texting receipts the moment I get them.

Client dinner? Before I sign the check, I photograph the receipt and text it to TextExpense. Done. Never think about it again.

Uber receipt in my email? Forward it. Done.

Office supplies? Snap it in the parking lot before I drive away.

Each one takes maybe fifteen seconds. No logging into a system. No categorizing. No uploading. Just text and forget.

When the Deadline Hits
