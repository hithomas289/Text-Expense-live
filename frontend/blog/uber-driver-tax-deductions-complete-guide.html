The Uber Driver's Tax Cheat Sheet: Every Deduction You're Probably Missing

You drove 847 miles last week. You know exactly how much Uber paid you.

But do you know how much you left on the table?

As a self-employed worker, deducting business expenses is critical to lower your taxes. The problem? Most drivers only claim the obvious stuff and miss hundreds (sometimes thousands) in legitimate write-offs.

Here's the complete list.

The Big One: Mileage

The largest tax deduction for most Uber drivers is the business use of a car.

For 2025, the IRS standard mileage rate is $0.70 per mile.

That means 1,000 business miles = $700 off your taxable income.

But here's what Uber and Lyft don't track: miles you drove waiting for a trip, the mileage you drove after dropping off passengers if you're waiting for another ride, and the mileage you drove before rides were canceled.

Those miles count. Track them.

The Deductions Most Drivers Miss

Your phone bill. You can deduct your phone plan's cost as a business expense‚Äîthe portion used for business. If you use your phone 40% for rideshare, that's 40% of your bill.

Phone accessories. Car chargers, cables, and phone holders are all tax deductible.

Car washes. Writing off your car mats, cleaning supplies and cleaning services, including car washes, is totally legit.

Passenger goodies. You can deduct the complimentary goodies that you offer your passengers like bottled waters, mints, and Halloween candy.

Music subscriptions. Spotify, Apple Music, Pandora, Sirius XM, and other subscriptions used to entertain passengers are write-offs.

Dashcams. Dashcams that are used as a security precaution are deductible.

Roadside assistance. A percentage of the fees for AAA or other roadside assistance programs are tax deductible based on the percentage of miles you drive for work.

Vehicle inspections. Vehicle inspections and background checks that are required for work are deductible.

Apps you pay for. Any work-related apps, including for navigation and taxes, can be written off.

Tolls and parking. Already paid through Uber? Still check‚Äîsome aren't reimbursed.

Oil changes and repairs. Oil changes, repairs, and regular checkups are all tax-deductible if you drive for work.

The Catch: You Need Receipts

Here's where most drivers blow it.

It is critical to track all these expenses and keep your receipts so you can claim all the deductions you deserve. You need to have detailed records to back up your claims.

No receipt? No deduction. The IRS doesn't accept "I remember buying it."

Every receipt you lose or misplace could be costing you money on your taxes. Especially because companies like Uber and Lyft can't do it for you.
